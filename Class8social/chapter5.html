Let's Recall

1. What was the demand of Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi that was refused by the British?

Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi wanted the East India Company to recognise her adopted son as the heir to the kingdom after the death of her husband. However, the Company, confident of its power, refused her plea. This was part of the "Doctrine of Lapse" policy.

2. What did the British do to protect the interests of those who converted to Christianity?

To protect the interests of Christian converts, the British passed a new law in 1850. This law made it easier for Indians to convert to Christianity and had a key provision:

  • The law allowed an Indian who had converted to Christianity to inherit the property of his ancestors. This was a major change, as traditionally, conversion could lead to losing this right.
3. What objections did the sepoys have to the new cartridges that they were asked to use?

The sepoys had strong religious objections to the new cartridges:

  • They came to believe that the cartridges were greased with the fat of cows and pigs.
  • For Hindu sepoys, the cow is sacred, so biting a cartridge greased with its fat would be an insult to their religion.
  • For Muslim sepoys, the pig is considered unclean, so biting a cartridge with its fat would also defile their religion.
  • Because the top of the cartridge had to be bitten off before use, both Hindu and Muslim sepoys felt this was a deliberate attempt by the British to destroy their religion and caste.
4. How did the last Mughal emperor live the last years of his life?

The last Mughal emperor, Bahadur Shah Zafar, lived his last years in exile and prison:

  • After the British recaptured Delhi, he was tried in court and sentenced to life imprisonment.
  • He and his wife, Begum Zinat Mahal, were sent to a prison in Rangoon (now Yangon, Myanmar) in October 1858.
  • Bahadur Shah Zafar died in the Rangoon jail in November 1862, far away from his homeland.

Let's Discuss

5. What could be the reasons for the confidence of the British rulers about their position in India before May 1857?

The British were very confident about their position in India before May 1857 due to several reasons:

  • Military Superiority: They had a powerful and well-equipped army that had defeated many Indian rulers and expanded their territory over a hundred years.
  • Political Dominance: They had successfully implemented policies like the 'subsidiary alliance' and the 'Doctrine of Lapse' to take control of many Indian kingdoms, such as Awadh.
  • Lack of Unity among Indians: The British had seen that Indian rulers often did not unite against them. They believed they could handle any small-scale mutiny or protest easily, as they had done in the past.
  • Belief in Their "Civilising Mission": Many British officials believed they were superior and that their rule was for the good of India, so they did not expect a large-scale, united opposition.
6. What impact did Bahadur Shah Zafar’s support to the rebellion have on the people and the ruling families?

Bahadur Shah Zafar’s decision to support the rebellion had a huge impact and changed the situation dramatically:

  • Legitimacy and Leadership: It gave the rebellion a legitimate leader. The Mughal dynasty had ruled for centuries, so when the emperor supported the uprising, it was no longer just a mutiny of soldiers but a wider war of independence.
  • Inspiration for Ruling Families: Many smaller rulers and chieftains, who had ruled on behalf of the Mughals, were inspired. They felt that if the Mughal emperor could rule again, they too could regain their territories and authority.
  • Hope and Confidence for the People: The emperor's support gave courage, hope, and confidence to the common people and sepoys to join the uprising. It presented an alternative to British rule and made the rebellion a nationwide movement in northern India.
7. How did the British succeed in securing the submission of the rebel landowners of Awadh?

The British used a two-pronged strategy of force and reward to secure the submission of rebel landowners in Awadh:

  • Military Force: The British used their military might to defeat the rebel forces. They recaptured key centres like Lucknow and killed or captured major rebel leaders. This broke the backbone of the rebellion.
  • Announcing Rewards and Amnesty: They tried to win back the loyalty of the people. They announced that loyal landholders would be allowed to continue to enjoy their traditional rights over their lands.
  • Promise of Safety: They told the rebels that if they submitted, and if they had not killed any white people, they would remain safe and their rights and claims to land would not be denied. This encouraged many landowners to stop fighting and surrender.
8. In what ways did the British change their policies as a result of the rebellion of 1857?

The rebellion of 1857 was a huge shock to the British, and they made several major policy changes to regain control and prevent future uprisings:

  • End of Company Rule: The powers of the East India Company were transferred to the British Crown through the Act of 1858. India was now to be directly ruled by the British government. The Governor-General was given the new title of Viceroy.
  • Promise to Ruling Chiefs: The British assured all ruling chiefs that their territory would never be annexed. They could pass on their kingdoms to their heirs, including adopted sons, but they had to acknowledge the British Queen as their supreme authority.
  • Reorganisation of the Army: The proportion of Indian soldiers was reduced, and the number of European soldiers was increased. They also started recruiting more soldiers from among the Gurkhas, Sikhs, and Pathans, instead of from Awadh and Bihar.
  • Respect for Indian Customs: The British decided to respect the customary religious and social practices of the people in India, promising not to interfere in them.
  • Protection for Landlords: New policies were made to protect landlords and zamindars and give them security of rights over their lands to ensure their loyalty.

Let's Do

9. Find out stories and songs remembered by people in your area or your family about San Sattavan ki Ladaai. What memories do people cherish about the great uprising?

This is a great activity to connect history with your own family and community! Here’s how you can do it:

  • Talk to Elders: Ask your grandparents or other elderly relatives if they have heard any stories or songs about the 1857 rebellion. In many parts of north India, it is remembered as the 'First War of Independence'.
  • Look for Local Heroes: Many regions had their own local leaders who fought against the British. You might discover stories about heroes from your own town or district.
  • Famous Songs and Poems: One very famous poem you can look for is "Jhansi ki Rani" by Subhadra Kumari Chauhan. Its lines, "Bundele Harbolon ke munh humne suni kahani thi, Khoob ladi mardani woh toh Jhansi wali Rani thi," are known all over India and cherish the memory of Rani Lakshmibai's bravery.
  • Common Memories: People often cherish memories of bravery, sacrifice for freedom, and the unity shown by Hindus and Muslims against a common enemy.
10. Find out more about Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi. In what ways would she have been an unusual woman for her times?

Rani Lakshmibai was indeed a very unusual and inspiring woman for the 19th century. You can find many stories about her bravery. Here are some reasons why she was considered unusual:

  • Fighting on the Battlefield: In a time when most women, especially queens, were expected to stay within the palace, she personally led her army in battle. She fought alongside her soldiers, which was very rare.
  • Mastery of Martial Arts: She was trained in horse riding, sword fighting, and using firearms from a young age, skills that were usually reserved for men and princes.
  • Challenging a Colonial Power: As a woman and a ruler of a small state, she directly challenged the mighty British empire. She refused to surrender her kingdom and became one of the most powerful symbols of resistance against British rule.
  • Administrative Skills: After her husband's death, she took charge of the administration of Jhansi and proved to be a capable and just ruler before the rebellion began.

Her courage and leadership have made her an icon of the Indian freedom struggle.
