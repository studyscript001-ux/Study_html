Let's Discuss

3. What problems did shifting cultivators face under British rule?

Shifting cultivators (or jhum cultivators) faced several serious problems under British rule:

  • Loss of Land: The British introduced land settlements to get a regular revenue. They measured the land and gave ownership rights to some, making others tenants. This disrupted the traditional system where land often belonged to the whole clan.
  • Restrictions on Movement: The British were uncomfortable with groups that moved around. They wanted them to settle down and become peasant cultivators, as settled people were easier to control and administer. This went against the very nature of shifting cultivation.
  • Forest Laws: The British declared forests as state property and reserved large areas. Shifting cultivators were banned from practising their traditional cultivation in these Reserved Forests.
  • Unsuccessful Settled Cultivation: The British effort to make them take up settled plough cultivation often failed. Jhum cultivators' fields did not produce good yields in dry areas with scarce water, leading to suffering.
4. How did the powers of tribal chiefs change under colonial rule?

The powers and authority of tribal chiefs changed completely under colonial rule. Before the British, they were powerful figures, but afterwards:

  • Loss of Administrative Power: They lost most of their administrative powers and were forced to follow laws made by British officials. They could no longer manage their territories and police their areas as they did before.
  • Forced to Pay Tribute: They had to pay tribute (a form of tax or gift) to the British.
  • Became Agents of the British: They were expected to discipline their own people on behalf of the British.
  • Loss of Authority: As a result, they lost the authority and respect they had once enjoyed amongst their people and were unable to fulfill their traditional functions. While they were allowed to keep their land titles, they were chiefs only in name.
5. What accounts for the anger of the tribals against the dikus?

The term "dikus" meant outsiders, and the tribals were angry with them for several reasons, as these outsiders were seen as the cause of their misery:

  • Moneylenders (Dikus): They gave loans to tribals at very high interest rates. This trapped the tribals in a cycle of debt and poverty, often leading to them losing their land.
  • Traders (Dikus): They came into the forests and bought forest produce at very cheap rates. For example, silk traders paid very little to the Santhal cocoon growers but sold the cocoons at huge profits.
  • Landlords and the British Government (Dikus): The new land policies of the British were destroying the traditional land system of the tribals. Hindu landlords and moneylenders were taking over their land, which the tribals had cultivated for generations.

6. What was Birsaâ€™s vision of a golden age? Why do you think such a vision appealed to the people of the region?

Birsa Munda's vision was to bring back a glorious past, which he called a "golden age" or satyug (the age of truth). In this golden age:

  • The Munda people would live an honest and good life, free from the oppression of dikus.
  • They would not kill their relatives and brethren.
  • They would construct embankments, tap natural springs, plant trees and orchards, and practise cultivation to earn their living.
  • They would work on their own land and be the masters of their own destiny.

This vision appealed to the people because:

  • It reminded them of a better past before their lives were turned upside down by forest laws, traders, and moneylenders.
  • Their familiar ways of life were disappearing, and their livelihoods were under threat. Birsa's vision gave them hope for a future free from suffering, debt, and outside control.
  • It was a call to restore their dignity and their ancestral rights to their land, which they felt were being taken away from them.
