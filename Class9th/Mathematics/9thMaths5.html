Question 1: Which of the following statements are true and which are false? Give reasons for your answers.

(i) Only one line can pass through a single point.

This statement is False.

According to Euclid's axioms, an infinite number of lines can pass through a single point.

A single point does not determine a unique line.

Hinglish Explanation: Yeh statement galat hai. Ek single point se infinite lines pass ho sakti hain. Euclid's axioms ke according, ek point se multiple lines pass ho sakti hain.

(ii) There are an infinite number of lines which pass through two distinct points.

This statement is False.

According to Euclid's Postulate 1 and Axiom 5.1, only one unique line can pass through two distinct points.

If more than one line passed through two points, it would violate the basic definition of a straight line.

Hinglish Explanation: Yeh statement bhi galat hai. Euclid's Postulate 1 ke according, do distinct points se sirf ek hi unique line pass ho sakti hai.

(iii) A terminated line can be produced indefinitely on both the sides.

This statement is True.

According to Euclid's Postulate 2, a terminated line (line segment) can be produced indefinitely on both sides to form a line.

This is a fundamental property of straight lines in Euclidean geometry.

Hinglish Explanation: Yeh statement sahi hai. Euclid's Postulate 2 ke according, ek terminated line (line segment) ko dono sides mein indefinitely extend kiya ja sakta hai.

(iv) If two circles are equal, then their radii are equal.

This statement is True.

According to Euclid's Postulate 3, a circle can be drawn with any center and any radius.

If two circles are equal (congruent), they must have the same size, which means their radii must be equal.

Hinglish Explanation: Yeh statement sahi hai. Agar do circles equal (congruent) hain, toh unki radii bhi equal honi chahiye. Euclid's Postulate 3 ke according, circle ko kisi bhi center aur radius se draw kiya ja sakta hai.

(v) In Fig. 5.9, if AB = PQ and PQ = XY, then AB = XY.

This statement is True.

According to Euclid's first axiom: "Things which are equal to the same thing are equal to one another."

Since AB = PQ and PQ = XY, therefore AB = XY.

Hinglish Explanation: Yeh statement sahi hai. Euclid's first axiom ke according: "Jo cheezein ek hi cheez ke equal hain, woh aapas mein bhi equal hoti hain." Yahan AB = PQ aur PQ = XY, isliye AB = XY.

Question 2: Give a definition for each of the following terms. Are there other terms that need to be defined first? What are they, and how might you define them?

(i) Parallel lines

Definition: Parallel lines are lines in a plane that do not meet; they are always the same distance apart and never intersect.

Terms needed first: To define parallel lines, we need to define "line", "plane", "distance", and "intersect".

How to define them:

  • Line: A line is a straight one-dimensional figure having no thickness and extending infinitely in both directions.
  • Plane: A plane is a flat, two-dimensional surface that extends infinitely far.
  • Distance: The shortest path between two points.
  • Intersect: When two lines cross each other at a point.
Hinglish Explanation: Parallel lines woh lines hain jo ek hi plane mein hain aur kabhi nahi milti, hamesha same distance par rehti hain. Is definition ke liye pehle "line", "plane", "distance", aur "intersect" ko define karna padega.

(ii) Perpendicular lines

Definition: Perpendicular lines are lines that intersect at right angles (90 degrees).

Terms needed first: To define perpendicular lines, we need to define "line", "intersect", and "right angle".

How to define them:

  • Line: As defined above.
  • Intersect: As defined above.
  • Right angle: An angle that measures exactly 90 degrees.
Hinglish Explanation: Perpendicular lines woh lines hain jo right angles (90 degrees) par intersect karti hain. Is definition ke liye pehle "line", "intersect", aur "right angle" ko define karna padega.

(iii) Line segment

Definition: A line segment is a part of a line that is bounded by two distinct end points and contains every point on the line between its endpoints.

Terms needed first: To define line segment, we need to define "line", "point", and "between".

How to define them:

  • Line: As defined above.
  • Point: A point is a location in space that has no dimension (no length, width, or height).
  • Between: A point C is between points A and B if all three points are collinear and AC + CB = AB.
Hinglish Explanation: Line segment line ka ek hissa hota hai jo do distinct end points ke beech mein hota hai. Is definition ke liye pehle "line", "point", aur "between" ko define karna padega.

(iv) Radius of a circle

Definition: The radius of a circle is a straight line from the center of the circle to any point on its circumference.

Terms needed first: To define radius, we need to define "circle", "center", "straight line", and "circumference".

How to define them:

  • Circle: A circle is a set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point called the center.
  • Center: The fixed point from which all points on a circle are equidistant.
  • Straight line: As defined in line above.
  • Circumference: The boundary line of a circle.
Hinglish Explanation: Radius circle ke center se circumference tak ki straight line hoti hai. Is definition ke liye pehle "circle", "center", "straight line", aur "circumference" ko define karna padega.

(v) Square

Definition: A square is a quadrilateral with all four sides equal in length and all four angles equal to 90 degrees.

Terms needed first: To define square, we need to define "quadrilateral", "side", "length", "angle", and "90 degrees".

How to define them:

  • Quadrilateral: A polygon with four sides and four angles.
  • Side: A line segment that forms part of a polygon.
  • Length: The measurement of something from end to end.
  • Angle: The figure formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint.
  • 90 degrees: The measure of a right angle.
Hinglish Explanation: Square ek quadrilateral hota hai jiske charo sides equal hote hain aur charo angles 90 degrees ke hote hain. Is definition ke liye pehle "quadrilateral", "side", "length", "angle", aur "90 degrees" ko define karna padega.

Question 3: Consider two 'postulates' given below:

(i) Given any two distinct points A and B, there exists a third point C which is in between A and B.

(ii) There exist at least three points that are not on the same line.

Do these postulates contain any undefined terms? Are these postulates consistent? Do they follow from Euclid's postulates? Explain.

Solution:

Undefined terms: Yes, these postulates contain undefined terms:

  • Point: This term is undefined in Euclidean geometry.
  • Line: This term is also undefined in Euclidean geometry.
  • Between: This term is not formally defined in Euclid's elements.

Consistency: Yes, these postulates are consistent with each other. They don't lead to any contradictions.

Following from Euclid's postulates: These postulates do not directly follow from Euclid's five postulates. They are additional assumptions about the nature of space.

Euclid's postulates don't explicitly guarantee that between any two points there exists a third point, or that there exist three non-collinear points.

These postulates help establish that space is continuous (not discrete) and that we're working in at least two dimensions.

Hinglish Explanation: In postulates mein undefined terms hain jaise "point", "line", aur "between". Yeh postulates consistent hain, matlab woh aapas mein contradict nahi karte. Yeh Euclid's postulates se directly follow nahi karte, balki additional assumptions hain space ki nature ke bare mein.

Question 7: Why is Axiom 5, in the list of Euclid's axioms, considered a 'universal truth'? (Note that the question is not about the fifth postulate.)

Solution:

Euclid's Axiom 5 states: "The whole is greater than the part."

This axiom is considered a 'universal truth' because:

  • It is self-evident and does not require proof.
  • It applies to all quantities and magnitudes, not just geometric figures.
  • It is consistent with our everyday experience and intuition.
  • It forms the basis for many other mathematical concepts and proofs.
  • It is independent of any specific geometric system or measurement units.

For example:

  • If we have a quantity A and it contains a part B, then A must be greater than B.
  • If B is a part of A, then we can write A = B + C for some C > 0, which clearly shows A > B.

This axiom is so fundamental that it's hard to imagine any consistent mathematical system where it wouldn't hold true.

Hinglish Explanation: Euclid's Axiom 5 kehta hai: "The whole is greater than the part." Yeh ek 'universal truth' hai kyunki yeh self-evident hai, proof ki zaroorat nahi hai. Yeh har tarah ki quantities par apply hota hai, sirf geometry tak limited nahi hai. Yeh hamari rozmarra ki experience aur intuition ke saath match karta hai. For example, agar kisi quantity A ka ek part B hai, toh A hamesha B se bada hoga.
