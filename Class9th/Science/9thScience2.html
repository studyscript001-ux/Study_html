Is Matter Around Us Pure?

1 Which separation techniques will you apply for the separation of the following?
(a) Sodium chloride from its solution in water
(b) Ammonium chloride from a mixture containing sodium chloride and ammonium chloride
(c) Small pieces of metal in the engine oil of a car
(d) Different pigments from an extract of flower petals
(e) Butter from curd
(f) Oil from water
(g) Tea leaves from tea
(h) Iron pins from sand
(i) Wheat grains from husk
(j) Fine mud particles suspended in water
Answer:
(a) Evaporation - Salt can be obtained by evaporating the water.
(b) Sublimation - Ammonium chloride sublimes on heating, leaving behind sodium chloride.
(c) Filtration - Metal pieces can be separated by filtration.
(d) Chromatography - Different pigments can be separated using chromatography.
(e) Centrifugation - Butter can be separated from curd by centrifugation.
(f) Separating funnel - Oil and water form two separate layers and can be separated using a separating funnel.
(g) Filtration - Tea leaves can be separated by filtration.
(h) Magnetic separation - Iron pins can be separated using a magnet.
(i) Winnowing - Wheat grains can be separated from husk by winnowing.
(j) Filtration/Decantation/Sedimentation - Fine mud particles can be separated by these methods.
2 Write the steps you would use for making tea. Use the words solution, solvent, solute, dissolve, soluble, insoluble, filtrate and residue.
Answer:
1. Take water in a pan (solvent).
2. Add tea leaves and sugar (solutes) to it.
3. Heat the mixture. Sugar is soluble in water and dissolves completely to form a solution.
4. Tea leaves are insoluble in water and do not dissolve.
5. Filter the mixture. The liquid that passes through the filter is the filtrate (tea), and the solid left on the filter paper is the residue (tea leaves).
6. Add milk to the filtrate and heat again to prepare the final tea solution.

4 Explain the following giving examples.
(a) Saturated solution
(b) Pure substance
(c) Colloid
(d) Suspension
Answer:
(a) Saturated solution: A solution in which no more solute can be dissolved at a given temperature. Example: When sugar stops dissolving in water at room temperature, we get a saturated sugar solution.

(b) Pure substance: A substance that consists of only one type of particles, either atoms or molecules, and has a fixed composition. Examples: Elements like gold, silver; compounds like water, sodium chloride.

(c) Colloid: A heterogeneous mixture where the particle size is too small to be seen with naked eyes but big enough to scatter light. Examples: Milk, fog, butter.

(d) Suspension: A heterogeneous mixture in which solute particles do not dissolve but remain suspended throughout the medium. Examples: Muddy water, chalk powder in water.

9 Which of the following will show "Tyndall effect"?
(a) Salt solution
(b) Milk
(c) Copper sulphate solution
(d) Starch solution
Answer:
Tyndall effect is shown by colloidal solutions where particles are big enough to scatter light.

Will show Tyndall effect: (b) Milk, (d) Starch solution

Will not show Tyndall effect: (a) Salt solution, (c) Copper sulphate solution (both are true solutions)
