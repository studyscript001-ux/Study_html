This is Jody's Fawn

Comprehension Check (Part I)

1. What had happened to Jody's father?

Jody's father, Penny, had been bitten by a rattlesnake.

2. How did the doe save Penny's life?

The doe's heart and liver were used to draw out the poison from Penny's body, which saved his life.

3. Why does Jody want to bring the fawn home?

Jody wants to bring the fawn home because he feels it would be ungrateful to leave it to starve after they killed its mother to save his father's life.

4. How does Jody know that the fawn is a male?

Jody knows the fawn is a male because its spots are all in a line. On a female fawn, the spots are scattered "every which way."

Comprehension Check (Part II)

1. Jody didn't want Mill-wheel with him for two reasons. What were they?

Jody didn't want Mill-wheel with him because:

  • If the fawn was dead or couldn't be found, he didn't want anyone to see his disappointment.
  • If the fawn was there, the meeting would be so special and secret that he didn't want to share it with anyone.

2. Why was Mill-wheel afraid to leave Jody alone?

Mill-wheel was afraid to leave Jody alone because he was worried Jody might get lost or get bitten by a snake like his father did.

Comprehension Check (Part III)

1. How did Jody bring the fawn back home?

Jody carried the fawn in his arms. When his arms got tired, he let the fawn follow him, and when it got tired, he carried it again.

2. Jody was filled with emotion after he found the fawn. Can you find at least three words or phrases which show how he felt?

Words/phrases that show Jody's emotions: "delirious", "enchanted", "light-headed with his joy".

3. How did the deer drink milk from the gourd?

Jody dipped his fingers in the milk and let the fawn suck them. Then he slowly lowered his fingers into the milk until the fawn learned to drink directly from the gourd.

4. Why didn't the fawn follow Jody up the steps as he had thought it would?

The fawn was probably too young and unsure about climbing steps, or it might have been frightened by the unfamiliar structure.

Working with the Text

1. Why did Penny Baxter allow Jody to go find the fawn and raise it?

Penny allowed Jody to find and raise the fawn because he felt it would be ungrateful to leave it to starve after they had killed its mother to save his life. He also recognized Jody's compassion and responsibility.

2. What did Doc Wilson mean when he said, "Nothing in the world ever comes quite free"?

Doc Wilson meant that everything has a cost or consequence. In this case, using the doe's life to save Penny's came with the responsibility of caring for the orphaned fawn.

3. How did Jody look after the fawn, after he accepted the responsibility for doing this?

Jody carefully carried the fawn home, fed it milk by first letting it suck his fingers and then teaching it to drink from a gourd, and showed great care and affection toward it.

4. How does Jody's mother react when she hears that he is going to bring the fawn home? Why does she react in this way?

Jody's mother is initially surprised and hesitant. She reacts this way because she's concerned about the practical aspects of caring for a wild animal and the additional responsibility it would bring to their already difficult life.

Speaking

1. Do you think it is right to kill an animal to save a human life?

This is a complex ethical question. In emergency situations like the one in the story, where a human life is at immediate risk, many people would argue that it is justified to kill an animal to save a human. However, we should also consider that every life is valuable, and we should look for alternatives whenever possible. In modern times, we have antivenom and other medical treatments that might reduce the need to kill animals in such situations.

2. How would you persuade your parents to let you keep a pet?

I would persuade my parents by:

  • Showing them I'm responsible by taking care of my current chores without being reminded
  • Researching about the pet's needs and costs
  • Offering to use my allowance for pet expenses
  • Promising to take full responsibility for the pet's care
  • Explaining how having a pet can teach responsibility and provide companionship
  • Suggesting a trial period to prove I can handle the responsibility

Writing

1. Describe your imaginary pet

My pet dog, Sparky, is a lively and enthusiastic companion who keeps me busy all day. He's frisky and playful, always ready for a game of fetch in the yard. Though he can be naughty sometimes - he often hides the newspaper and drinks up milk if left unattended - his loyalty and happy nature make up for these small mischiefs. After a day of play, he loves to roll in mud, which means bath time is a regular event in our house. Despite the challenges, having Sparky as a friend is wonderful. He's always there to share both joyful moments and quiet times, making every day brighter with his presence.

2. Our relationship with nature

(i) Natural resources we use: water, air, minerals, forests, fossil fuels, land for agriculture, plants for food and medicine, animals for food and other products.

(ii) Our relationship with nature is one of taking without giving back enough. We depend on nature for everything we need to live, from the air we breathe to the food we eat. However, we often exploit natural resources without considering the consequences. We pollute rivers, cut down forests, and harm wildlife habitats. It's important that we learn to live in harmony with nature by conserving resources, reducing pollution, and protecting ecosystems. We should remember that we are part of nature, not separate from it, and our well-being is directly connected to the health of our planet.

3. What to do if a snake bites you

If a snake bites you, it's important to stay calm and seek medical help immediately. Not all snakes are poisonous, but it's best to treat every bite as potentially dangerous. Keep the bitten area still and positioned below the level of your heart. Remove any tight clothing or jewelry near the bite. Don't try to suck out the venom or cut the wound. Don't apply a tourniquet or ice. Try to remember the snake's appearance to help doctors identify it and provide the right antivenom if needed. While waiting for help, keep the person calm and still to slow the spread of venom if it is a poisonous snake.
