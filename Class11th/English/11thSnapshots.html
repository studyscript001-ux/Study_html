1. The Summer of the Beautiful White Horse

Q1. You will probably agree that this story does not have breathless adventure and exciting action. Then what in your opinion makes it interesting?
The story's interest lies not in fast-paced action but in its gentle humour, moral complexity, and the portrayal of human values. The two boys, Aram and Mourad, belong to the Garoghlanian tribe known for honesty. Despite their poverty, they take the horse not out of greed but out of pure love for riding. Their internal conflict – enjoying the horse while knowing it is stolen – creates a subtle tension. The way they return the horse without being caught, and the farmer John Byro’s trust in their honesty, makes the story touching. The character of Uncle Khosrove, with his comic roar “It is no harm; pay no attention to it,” adds humour. The story celebrates the triumph of innocence and family pride over material temptation.
Q2. Did the boys return the horse because they were conscience-stricken or because they were afraid?
The boys returned the horse primarily because they were conscience‑stricken, not because of fear. Mourad and Aram belonged to the Garoghlanian tribe, which had been famous for honesty for eleven centuries. When John Byro, the owner, visited their house and said he would swear the horse was his but for the family’s reputation of honesty, the boys felt ashamed. Their conscience pricked them. Mourad, though considered “crazy,” had a strong sense of tribal honour. They returned the horse secretly, and John Byro later found it healthier and better‑tempered. If they had been afraid, they would have hidden it longer or made excuses. Their action was driven by their inner sense of right and wrong.
Q3. The story begins in a mood of nostalgia. Can you narrate some incident from your childhood that might make an interesting story?
[Sample answer] When I was about ten years old, my cousin and I found a wounded sparrow in our backyard. We named it “Chirpy” and secretly kept it in an old shoebox. Every day after school, we would feed it grains and water. My parents did not allow pets, so we hid it in the garden shed. One day Chirpy flew away, but before leaving, it circled over our heads three times. That simple incident taught me about love, letting go, and the joy of helping a living creature. It remains a warm memory of childhood innocence.
Q4. Locate Armenia and Assyria on the atlas. Prepare a write‑up on the Garoghlanian tribes.
Armenia is a landlocked country in the South Caucasus region of Eurasia, bordered by Turkey, Georgia, Azerbaijan, and Iran. Its capital is Yerevan. Assyria was an ancient Mesopotamian civilization centred in northern Iraq (present‑day Mosul region). The Garoghlanian tribe (fictional but based on Armenian values) is portrayed as poverty‑stricken yet extremely honest, proud, and family‑oriented. They have lived for centuries with a code of honour: “proud first, honest next, and after that we believed in right and wrong.” They are known for their strong family bonds, love for horses, and a touch of eccentricity (like Uncle Khosrove’s roaring). Despite hardships, they never steal or cheat. The story celebrates these traits as their true wealth.

2. The Address – Marga Minco

Q1. Why did the narrator wait a long time before going to Mrs Dorling’s house?
After the Liberation (end of World War II), the narrator was not interested in material possessions. She was afraid of being confronted with objects that belonged to her mother, whose life had been lost. The memories attached to those things were painful. Also, the house where her mother’s belongings were stored reminded her of the life that no longer existed. She says: “I was absolutely not interested in all that stored stuff, and naturally I was also rather afraid of it.” Over time, as life became more normal, curiosity grew, and she finally decided to go.
Q2. How did the narrator recognise Mrs Dorling at the door?
The narrator recognised Mrs Dorling because she was wearing her mother’s green knitted cardigan. The wooden buttons were rather pale from washing. The narrator had seen Mrs Dorling only once, years ago, but the cardigan was unmistakable. That object triggered recognition and confirmed that she was at the right address.
Q3. Why did the narrator decide to leave all the belongings behind at the end?
When the narrator finally entered Mrs Dorling’s house, she found her mother’s precious things – the cutlery, table‑cloth, antique plates – arranged in a tasteless, vulgar manner. The objects had lost their warmth and meaning. They were now just “things” in a strange, ugly room. The narrator realised that memories cannot be reclaimed by taking back objects. She felt oppressed by the strange atmosphere. She said, “I didn’t want to upset myself with the sight of streets and houses full of memories from a precious time.” So she decided to leave everything behind and move on, saying, “I resolved to forget the address.” It was an act of letting go of the past.
Q4. What does the title “The Address” signify?
The title “The Address” refers to Mrs Dorling’s house at Number 46, Marconi Street – the place where the narrator’s mother had sent all her valuable belongings for safekeeping during the war. It symbolises a link to the past, to the narrator’s mother and her lost world. However, at the end, the narrator decides to forget the address, signifying her decision to break free from painful memories and not let material objects define her relationship with her mother.

3. Mother’s Day – J.B. Priestley

Q1. (i) What are the issues raised in the play? (ii) Do you think it caricatures these issues? Are the problems genuine? How does the play resolve them? Do you agree with the resolution?
(i) The play raises issues of a mother’s unrecognised labour, lack of respect from family members, gender roles, and the need for domestic workers (especially housewives) to be valued. Mrs Pearson is taken for granted – her husband, son, and daughter treat her like a servant.
(ii) The play uses mild caricature (e.g., pompous George, spoilt Doris and Cyril) to highlight genuine problems. Many mothers in the 1950s (and even today) face similar neglect. The resolution comes through Mrs Fitzgerald swapping personalities with Mrs Pearson and giving the family a harsh lesson. In the end, the family agrees to stay home, play rummy, and help with supper.
Agreement: The resolution is effective as a theatrical device, though real‑life change requires more than one evening. Still, it shows that firmness and self‑respect can transform family dynamics.
Q2. If you were to write about these issues today, what incidents/examples would you think of as relevant?
Today, relevant incidents include: working mothers who still do most household chores; fathers who never cook or clean; teenage children who expect meals without thanking; the emotional labour of managing family schedules. Examples: a mother working from home but also making breakfast, packing lunches, and doing laundry while others watch TV; a daughter expecting her mother to iron her dress at the last moment; a husband who never asks how his wife’s day was. The digital age adds new dimensions – mothers often manage children’s online schooling while being ignored.
Q3. Is drama a good medium for conveying a social message? Discuss.
Yes, drama is an excellent medium for social messages because it combines dialogue, action, and emotion in a live or recorded performance. It can make the audience laugh, cry, or think. Plays like Mother’s Day use humour to highlight serious issues without being preachy. Drama has immediacy – characters speak directly to the audience. It also allows for exaggeration (caricature) to drive the point home. However, the message must be integrated naturally into the plot, not forced. When done well, drama can influence public opinion and even inspire social change.
Q4. Read the play out in parts. Enact the play on a suitable occasion. (Activity – no written answer required)
Activity to be performed in class. Students can take roles: Mrs Pearson, Mrs Fitzgerald, George, Doris, Cyril. The play can be enacted on Mother’s Day or during a school cultural event. Focus on contrasting voices – fluttering vs deep Irish voice – and the magical swap scene.
Q5. Discuss in groups plays or films with a strong message of social reform that you have watched.
[Sample group discussion points] Examples: “Pink” (Hindi film) – message on consent and women’s rights. “To Kill a Mockingbird” (play/film) – racial justice. “Article 15” – caste discrimination. “The Vagina Monologues” – women’s issues. In theatre, “Hayavadana” (Girish Karnad) questions identity and gender. These works use powerful storytelling to provoke thought and change attitudes.

4. Birth – A.J. Cronin

Q1. How did Andrew know that the child was stillborn?
When Andrew finally delivered the baby, he saw that the child was limp, white, and lifeless. The body was “white and soft as tallow”, the limbs seemed boneless, and there was no breathing or heartbeat. The whiteness indicated asphyxia pallida – a severe lack of oxygen at birth. The midwife also declared, “It’s stillborn.” Andrew’s medical training told him the baby was not breathing and appeared dead.
Q2. Why did Andrew decide to revive the child even after half an hour of effort?
Andrew was a dedicated doctor with a strong sense of duty. Despite the midwife’s advice to give up, he could not accept defeat. He remembered a case he had seen at the Samaritan hospital where a special method of resuscitation was used. Moreover, he thought of Joe Morgan’s long wait for a child and the old grandmother’s longing for a grandchild. His persistence was driven by compassion, professional pride, and the belief that where there is life, there is hope. He kept alternating between hot and cold water baths and using artificial respiration until he succeeded.
Q3. What was the dilemma Andrew faced immediately after the child was born?
Andrew faced a terrible dilemma: the mother, Susan Morgan, was collapsed and nearly pulseless, needing immediate attention. At the same time, the newborn child was lifeless and needed resuscitation. He could not attend to both simultaneously. He had to choose between saving the mother or trying to revive the child. Instinctively, he gave the child to the nurse and first treated the mother with an injection to strengthen her heart. Then he turned to the child. The dilemma was urgent and life‑critical.
Q4. What saved the child’s life? Describe the method used by Andrew.
Andrew used a combination of hot and cold water baths and artificial respiration. He plunged the child alternately into icy cold water and then into hot water (as hot as his hand could bear). He also laid the child on a blanket and performed the “special method of respiration” – compressing and releasing the tiny chest. After nearly half an hour of persistent effort, the child’s chest gave a convulsive heave, then another, and finally the baby began to breathe and cry. The sudden temperature changes and rhythmic pressure stimulated the respiratory centre.
Q5. How did Andrew feel after the successful revival of the child?
Andrew felt weak, dazed, and almost giddy with relief and joy. The narrator says: “The sense of life, springing beneath his fingers after all that unavailing striving, was so exquisite it almost made him faint.” He had worked frantically for a long time, and success felt like a miracle. He mechanically wrung out his sleeve and pulled on his jacket, too exhausted for any dramatic celebration. Yet deep inside, it was a turning point in his career and gave him confidence.

5. The Tale of Melon City – Vikram Seth

Q1. Narrate The Tale of Melon City in your own words.
Once there was a just and placid king who ordered an arch to be built across the main thoroughfare. When the arch was completed, the king rode under it but his crown was knocked off because the arch was too low. The king ordered the chief builder to be hanged. The builder blamed the workmen, the workmen blamed the masons for wrong‑sized bricks, the masons blamed the architect. The architect revealed that the king himself had made amendments to the plans. The king, being just, could not punish himself. He called for the wisest man, who was so old he could neither walk nor see. The wise man declared that the arch itself should be hanged. A councillor objected that the arch had touched the king’s head, so how could it be hanged? The crowd grew restless and demanded a hanging. The king, to please the crowd, ordered that whoever was exactly the right height to fit the noose should be hanged. Only the king himself fit the noose. So by royal decree, the king was hanged. The ministers then proclaimed that the next person to pass the city gate would become king. An idiot passed by, and when asked who should be king, he replied “A melon.” So they crowned a melon.
Q2. What impression would you form of a state where the king was just and placid?
A just and placid king sounds ideal, but the poem shows that such a king can be absurdly literal in his justice. He refuses to punish anyone unfairly, yet he ends up hanging himself because he follows the “wisdom” of a foolish old man and the demands of a mob. A state with a just king should be orderly, but here justice becomes mechanical and ridiculous. The king’s placid nature makes him weak – he cannot take a firm decision and is swayed by ministers and crowd. So the impression is that even good qualities like justice and placidity, when taken to extremes without common sense, can lead to chaos.
Q3. How, according to you, can peace and liberty be maintained in a state?
Peace and liberty require a balance of wise leadership, rule of law, active citizen participation, and respect for fundamental rights. Leaders should be just but also practical and decisive. Laws must be clear, fair, and applied equally. There should be freedom of speech and expression, but also responsibility. Institutions like an independent judiciary and a free press help maintain checks and balances. Most importantly, the state should avoid blind adherence to tradition or mob rule, as seen in the poem. Education and rational thinking among citizens prevent foolish decisions like crowning a melon.
Q4. Suggest a few instances in the poem which highlight humour and irony.
Humour: The wise man is so old that he cannot walk or see – yet his advice is taken seriously. The idea of hanging an arch. The king measuring every man to find the exact height for the noose – and only he fits. The ministers’ relieved comment: “Thank Goodness we found someone” after hanging their own king. The idiot’s reply “A melon” to every question, and the ministers actually crowning a melon.
Irony: The king orders the arch built for his own glory, but it knocks off his crown. The just king ends up being hanged because of his excessive sense of justice. The wisest man gives the silliest advice. The people demand a hanging, and the only victim is the king who wanted to please them. The ministers shout “Long live the King!” after he is dead.
Q5. The poem is narrated in verse form. Find similar examples in your language. (Open‑ended)
[Sample answer] In Hindi, similar narrative poems include “Panch Parmeshwar” by Munshi Premchand (prose, but has verse adaptations) and folk tales like “Sone ka Anda” told in couplets. In Urdu, the genre of “masnavi” tells long stories in rhyming couplets, e.g., “Zehar-e-Ishq” by Mirza Ghalib. In English, narrative verse includes “The Rime of the Ancient Mariner” by Coleridge and “The Hunting of the Snark” by Lewis Carroll. These use rhyme and rhythm to make the story memorable and entertaining.
