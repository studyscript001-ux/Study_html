Understanding the Text (Silk Road)

I. Give reasons for the following statements.
1. The article has been titled 'Silk Road'.
The title 'Silk Road' is appropriate because the author's journey follows the ancient trade route that connected Lhasa to Kashmir, passing through the Tibetan plateau. This route was historically part of the Silk Road, along which silk, spices, and other goods were traded, and Buddhism spread from India to China and beyond. The author's journey to Mount Kailash follows this same path, and he even mentions modern exports like plastic bags traveling along the Silk Road today.

2. Tibetan mastiffs were popular in China's imperial courts.
Tibetan mastiffs were popular in China's imperial courts because they were ferocious, fearless, and loyal dogs. Their massive size, dark coats, and protective nature made them excellent hunting dogs and guardians. They were brought along the Silk Road as tribute from Tibet to Chinese emperors, who valued them for their strength and courage.

3. The author's experience at Hor was in stark contrast to earlier accounts of the place.
Earlier travellers like Japanese monk Ekai Kawaguchi (1900) and Swede Sven Hedin (1902) were deeply moved by the sanctity of Lake Manasarovar – Kawaguchi burst into tears, and even Hedin (not prone to sentimental outbursts) was affected. However, when the author visited Hor, he found it a "grim, miserable place" with no vegetation, accumulated refuse, badly painted concrete buildings, and a dirty cafe. The stark contrast highlights how the place has deteriorated over time.

4. The author was disappointed with Darchen.
The author was disappointed with Darchen because it was dusty, partially derelict, and full of rubble and refuse. More importantly, there were no pilgrims despite it being the beginning of the pilgrimage season. He had come to do the kora (circumambulation) of Mount Kailash, but without fellow travellers, he felt uncertain about doing it alone. His sleeping difficulties and altitude sickness added to his disappointment.

5. The author thought that his positive thinking strategy worked well after all.
The author had been trying to maintain a positive outlook despite his troubles. When he met Norbu in the cafe, his spirits lifted. Norbu was a Tibetan academic from Beijing who also wanted to do the kora. He suggested they form a team – "Two academics who have escaped from the library." This unexpected companionship gave the author hope and made him feel that his positive thinking strategy was finally working.
II. Briefly comment on the following.
1. The purpose of the author's journey to Mount Kailash.
The author travelled to Mount Kailash to complete the kora – the sacred circumambulation of the mountain, which is considered holy by Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Bonpos. He wanted to experience the pilgrimage firsthand, possibly for personal spiritual reasons or as part of his travel writing. The journey involved crossing high mountain passes, enduring harsh weather, and dealing with altitude sickness.

2. The author's physical condition in Darchen.
In Darchen (elevation 4,760 metres), the author suffered from a severe cold, blocked sinuses, and breathing difficulties. He experienced frightening nights where he couldn't lie down to sleep because his chest felt heavy and he was afraid he might not wake up. He consulted a Tibetan doctor who diagnosed a cold and altitude effects, prescribing Tibetan medicine (brown powder and small brown pellets) that eventually helped him sleep soundly.

3. The author's meeting with Norbu.
The author met Norbu in Darchen's only cafe. Norbu was a Tibetan working at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences in Beijing. He had written academic papers about the Kailash kora but had never actually done it. He was also alone and ill-equipped (overweight, not a practising Buddhist). They decided to become a team, and Norbu's enthusiasm and sense of humour lifted the author's spirits.

4. Tsetan's support to the author during the journey.
Tsetan was the author's driver and guide. He provided crucial support by navigating treacherous mountain passes, dealing with snow blockages, fixing punctures, and ensuring the author's safety. He also took the author to the Darchen medical college when he fell ill. Tsetan was knowledgeable about the terrain and culture, and his calm, practical assistance made the difficult journey possible.

5. "As a Buddhist, he told me, he knew that it didn't really matter if I passed away, but he thought it would be bad for business."
This statement by Tsetan reflects Buddhist philosophy – that death is not the end and that the cycle of rebirth continues. However, Tsetan adds a practical, humorous twist: while the author's death wouldn't matter spiritually, it would be bad for his business (as a driver/guide). This shows Tsetan's wit and his ability to lighten serious moments.

Talking about the Text (Silk Road)

1. The sensitive behaviour of hill-folk.
The hill-folk (drokbas – Tibetan nomads) displayed sensitive and hospitable behaviour. They lived in splendid isolation in their dark tents, guarded by ferocious mastiffs. When the author's car passed, they would pause and stare, occasionally waving. Their sheep would take evasive action when the vehicle came close. The drokbas gave the author a sheepskin coat (drokba) as a farewell gift. Despite the harsh environment, they showed kindness and curiosity towards strangers. Their sensitivity is also seen in how they respect traditions – for example, walking clockwise around the cairn of rocks at the mountain pass.
2. The reasons why people willingly undergo the travails of difficult journeys.
People willingly undertake difficult journeys like the Kailash kora for several reasons:
• Religious/spiritual reasons: For Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Bonpos, circumambulating Mount Kailash is believed to bring spiritual merit, cleanse sins, and lead to enlightenment.
• Adventure and challenge: Some seek the thrill of testing themselves against nature's harshest conditions.
• Cultural exploration: Travellers like the author want to experience and document remote cultures and landscapes.
• Personal transformation: Overcoming physical and mental challenges can lead to personal growth and self-discovery.
• Tradition and duty: Some pilgrims undertake the journey as part of family or religious tradition.
3. The accounts of exotic places in legends and the reality.
There is often a gap between legendary accounts and reality. For example, Lake Manasarovar was described in ancient Hindu and Buddhist cosmology as a sacred, venerated source of four great rivers. Earlier travellers like Kawaguchi and Hedin were moved to tears by its sanctity. However, when the author visited Hor on its shore, he found a grim, miserable town with dust, rocks, refuse, and a dirty cafe. The reality was disappointing compared to the legendary accounts. Similarly, Darchen was described as bustling with pilgrims, but the author found it empty and derelict. This contrast shows that places change over time and that romanticised legends may not match present-day conditions.

Thinking about Language (Silk Road)

1. Notice the kind of English Tsetan uses while talking to the author. How do you think he picked it up?
Tsetan uses a simple, functional form of English, often using phrases like "No problem, sir", "Not good, sir", "Maybe, sir". His English is broken but effective for communication. He likely picked it up through interactions with tourists and travellers over the years. As a driver and guide in a remote area, he would have learned practical English on the job – listening to passengers, picking up common phrases, and gradually building a working vocabulary. He may not have had formal education in English.
2. What do the following utterances indicate? (i) "I told her, through Daniel ..." (ii) "It's a cold," he said finally through Tsetan.
Both utterances indicate that the author and the person he was speaking to did not share a common language, so they needed an interpreter.
(i) "I told her, through Daniel" – The author spoke to Lhamo, but Daniel acted as translator (likely translating English to Tibetan).
(ii) "It's a cold," he said finally through Tsetan – The Tibetan doctor spoke to Tsetan in Tibetan, and Tsetan translated the diagnosis into English for the author. The word 'through' indicates that communication was mediated by a third person.
3. Guess the meaning of the following words. kora, drokba, kyang. In which language are these words found?
• kora: A sacred circumambulation or pilgrimage around a holy site (especially Mount Kailash) – a ritual walk performed by Buddhists and Hindus.
• drokba: A Tibetan nomad or herder (also spelled 'drokpa') – someone who lives in the high-altitude grasslands of Tibet, tending flocks of sheep, goats, or yaks.
• kyang: The Tibetan wild ass (Equus kiang) – a large, wild equid native to the Tibetan Plateau.
These words are from the Tibetan language.

Summary of the Tethys Ocean (based on the extract and further knowledge):
The Tethys Ocean was an ancient ocean that existed between the continents of Gondwana (south) and Laurasia (north) during the Mesozoic Era (about 250 to 65 million years ago). It covered much of what is now the Mediterranean region, the Middle East, and the Himalayas. When the Indian tectonic plate collided with the Eurasian plate (about 50 million years ago), the Tethys Ocean floor was forced upward, creating the Himalayan mountain range and the Tibetan Plateau. Fossils of marine creatures found in the Himalayas prove that these rocks were once underwater. Remnants of the Tethys Ocean include the Mediterranean Sea, the Black Sea, and the salt flats and brackish lakes of Tibet (like those mentioned in the chapter). The term 'Tethys' was named by geologist Eduard Suess after the Greek goddess of the sea.

Father to Son (Poem) - Think It Out

1. Does the poem talk of an exclusively personal experience or is it fairly universal?
The poem deals with a universal experience – the generation gap and the breakdown of communication between parents and children. While the poet (Elizabeth Jennings) draws on her personal feelings, the situation of a father feeling estranged from his son is common across cultures and time periods. Many parents feel that they "know nothing" of their grown children, despite having lived together for years. The poem captures the universal pain of a parent who loves their child but cannot connect with them, and the child's inability to explain their own anger. This theme resonates with families worldwide.
2. How is the father's helplessness brought out in the poem?
The father's helplessness is brought out through several lines and images:
• "I do not understand this child" – admission of complete incomprehension.
• "Though we have lived together now / In the same house for years" – despite physical proximity, there is emotional distance.
• "I know / Nothing of him" – stark confession of ignorance.
• "We speak like strangers, there's no sign / Of understanding in the air" – communication is empty and meaningless.
• "Silence surrounds us" – the failure to connect creates a painful void.
• The father wishes his son would be "prodigal" (like the Biblical prodigal son) and return home, showing he would forgive anything.
• "We each put out an empty hand, / Longing for something to forgive" – both want to reconcile but cannot bridge the gap.
3. Identify the phrases and lines that indicate distance between father and son.
Phrases and lines indicating distance:
• "I do not understand this child"
• "I know / Nothing of him"
• "We speak like strangers"
• "There's no sign / Of understanding in the air"
• "Silence surrounds us"
• "What he loves I cannot share"
• "He speaks: I cannot understand / Myself" – the son cannot understand his own feelings
• "We each put out an empty hand" – reaching out but not connecting
• "Longing for something to forgive" – both want reconciliation but don't know how to achieve it
4. Does the poem have a consistent rhyme scheme?
The poem does not have a completely consistent rhyme scheme, but it uses occasional rhymes and near-rhymes. For example:
• In stanza 1: 'years' and 'air' (near rhyme), 'small' and 'all' (full rhyme), 'mine' and 'sign' (full rhyme).
• Stanza 2: 'share' and 'air' (full rhyme), 'would' and 'could' (full rhyme), 'move' and 'too' (near rhyme).
• Stanza 3: 'live' and 'forgive' (near rhyme), 'grief' and 'hand' (no rhyme).
The rhyme scheme is irregular and free-verse-like, which mirrors the unsettled, uncomfortable emotions of the father-son relationship. The lack of strict pattern emphasises the lack of harmony between the two.
