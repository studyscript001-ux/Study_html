Understanding the Text (The Ailing Planet)

1. Locate the lines in the text that support the title 'The Ailing Planet'.
The following lines from the text support the title 'The Ailing Planet':
• "The earth's vital signs reveal a patient in declining health."
• "In large areas of the world, human claims on these systems are reaching an unsustainable level, a point where their productivity is being impaired."
• "When this happens, fisheries collapse, forests disappear, grasslands are converted into barren wastelands, and croplands deteriorate."
• "The world's ancient patrimony of tropical forests is now eroding at the rate of forty to fifty million acres a year."
• "A recent report of our Parliament's Estimates Committee has highlighted the near catastrophic depletion of India's forests over the last four decades."
2. What does the notice 'The world's most dangerous animal' at a cage in the zoo at Lusaka, Zambia, signify?
The notice 'The world's most dangerous animal' is placed outside a cage that contains no animal but a mirror. When people look inside, they see their own reflection. This signifies that human beings are the most dangerous animals on Earth. It highlights that humans, through their activities like overconsumption, deforestation, pollution, and population growth, are responsible for the degradation of the environment. The mirror forces people to introspect and realise that they themselves are the greatest threat to the planet's survival.
3. How are the earth's principal biological systems being depleted?
The earth's four principal biological systems – fisheries, forests, grasslands, and croplands – are being depleted in the following ways:
• Fisheries: Over-fishing is common, leading to collapse of fisheries in many areas.
• Forests: Tropical forests are being destroyed at the rate of 40-50 million acres per year. In poor countries, forests are decimated for firewood.
• Grasslands: They are being converted into barren wastelands due to overgrazing and unsustainable use.
• Croplands: They deteriorate due to loss of fertility, and dung that could be used as natural fertiliser is being burnt as fuel.
Human claims on these systems have reached unsustainable levels, impairing their productivity.
4. Why does the author aver that the growth of world population is one of the strongest factors distorting the future of human society?
The author states that population growth is a strong distorting factor because:
• It took over a million years to reach the first billion (around 1800), but the next billion was added by 1900, and the twentieth century added 3.7 billion more.
• The present world population is 5.7 billion, increasing by one million every four days.
• More children in poor families condemn them to remain poor – "the rich get richer, and the poor beget children which condemns them to remain poor."
• Fertility falls with development (education, health, income), so development is the best contraceptive, but development itself is hindered by population growth.
• In India, the population of 920 million (more than Africa and South America combined) means hope would die in "hungry hutments" unless population control is given top priority.

Talking about the Text (The Ailing Planet)

1. Laws are never respected nor enforced in India. (Discuss in groups)
Arguments in favour: The author points out that despite Article 48A of the Constitution directing the State to protect the environment, laws are rarely enforced. He gives examples: casteism, untouchability, and bonded labour are constitutionally abolished yet flourish shamelessly. Forest laws are violated, and the actual loss of forests is eight times the government statistics.
Arguments against: Some laws are respected and enforced, e.g., traffic regulations in cities, income tax laws. The judiciary has become active through Public Interest Litigations (PILs) to enforce environmental laws. However, the author's overall concern about weak enforcement remains valid.
2. "Are we to leave our successors a scorched planet of advancing deserts, impoverished landscapes and an ailing environment?"
This question from the Brandt Report highlights the moral responsibility of the current generation towards future generations. If we continue depleting resources unsustainably – cutting forests, over-fishing, degrading croplands – we will leave behind a barren, polluted, and uninhabitable planet. The question challenges us to change our ways and adopt sustainable development so that our children and grandchildren inherit a healthy Earth, not a "scorched planet".
3. "We have not inherited this earth from our forefathers; we have borrowed it from our children."
This powerful statement by Lester Brown redefines our relationship with the planet. Traditionally, we think we have inherited the Earth from our ancestors. But Brown reverses this: we have borrowed it from future generations. This means we are merely temporary custodians or trustees. We have no right to destroy or deplete resources because they belong to those who will come after us. This perspective encourages sustainable development – using resources today without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.
4. The problems of overpopulation that directly affect our everyday life.
Overpopulation affects everyday life in many ways:
• Shortage of resources: Water scarcity, electricity cuts, fuel shortages.
• Traffic and overcrowding: Congestion on roads, crowded public transport, long queues.
• Housing problems: Expensive real estate, slums, lack of affordable housing.
• Unemployment: More job seekers than available positions.
• Pressure on education and healthcare: Overcrowded schools, long waiting lists for hospitals.
• Pollution: More vehicles, more waste, degraded air and water quality.
• Food prices: Rising demand leads to inflation.

Thinking about Language

The phrase 'inter alia' meaning 'among other things' is one of the many Latin expressions commonly used in English. Find out what these Latin phrases mean.
1. prima facie: At first sight; based on first impression; accepted as correct until proved otherwise.
2. ad hoc: Created or done for a particular purpose as necessary; makeshift.
3. in camera: In private; (of a legal case) heard in secret, not in open court.
4. ad infinitum: Endlessly; forever; without limit.
5. mutatis mutandis: The necessary changes having been made; with the respective differences considered.
6. caveat: A warning or proviso; a legal notice to stop proceedings.
7. tabula rasa: Blank slate; the mind before it receives impressions from experience.

Working with Words

I. Locate the following phrases in the text and study their connotation.
1. gripped the imagination of: Captured the interest and attention of people completely. (e.g., "the Green Movement gripped the imagination of the entire human race")
2. dawned upon: Became gradually understood or realised. (e.g., "a new awareness has now dawned upon the most dangerous animal")
3. ushered in: Brought in; marked the beginning of (a new period). (e.g., "The emerging new world vision has ushered in the Era of Responsibility")
4. passed into current coin: Became a commonly used phrase or expression. (e.g., Margaret Thatcher's words "passed decisively into the current coin of English usage")
5. passport of the future: Something that will enable entry into or guarantee a better future. (e.g., "The environmental problem... is our passport for the future")
II. Write pairs of sentences using each word in the literal as well as the figurative sense: grip, dawn, usher, coin, passport.
Grip:
• Literal: The child tightened his grip on his mother's hand.
• Figurative: Fear gripped the entire city during the terrorist attack.

Dawn:
• Literal: We woke up at dawn to watch the sunrise.
• Figurative: The truth finally dawned upon him after seeing the evidence.

Usher:
• Literal: The attendant ushered the guests into the hall.
• Figurative: The Industrial Revolution ushered in a new era of technology.

Coin:
• Literal: He tossed a coin into the fountain for good luck.
• Figurative: The phrase 'sustainable development' has now passed into common coin.

Passport:
• Literal: You need a valid passport to travel abroad.
• Figurative: A good education is a passport to a successful career.

Childhood (Poem) - Think It Out

1. Identify the stanza that talks of each of the following: individuality, rationalism, hypocrisy.
• Individuality: Third stanza – "Was it when I found my mind was really mine, To use whichever way I choose, Producing thoughts that were not those of other people But my own, and mine alone"
• Rationalism: First stanza – "Was it the time I realised that Hell and Heaven, Could not be found in Geography, And therefore could not be" – realising that religious concepts cannot be located on a map, showing logical/scientific thinking.
• Hypocrisy: Second stanza – "Was it the time I realised that adults were not all they seemed to be, They talked of love and preached of love, But did not act so lovingly" – adults preach one thing but practise another.
2. What according to the poem is involved in the process of growing up?
According to the poem, growing up involves:
• Loss of innocence and faith in religious concepts: Realising that Heaven and Hell are not physical places that can be found on a map.
• Recognition of adult hypocrisy: Understanding that adults do not practise what they preach; they talk and preach about love but do not act lovingly.
• Development of individuality: Discovering that one's mind is one's own and can produce independent thoughts, not just those of others.
• Rational and logical thinking: Questioning beliefs and forming one's own understanding based on reason.
3. What is the poet's feeling towards childhood?
The poet feels a sense of nostalgia and loss for childhood. He repeatedly asks "When did my childhood go?" suggesting that he misses the innocence and simplicity of that phase. He realises that childhood has disappeared without his noticing. In the final stanza, he says childhood "went to some forgotten place, That's hidden in an infant's face" – implying that childhood can only be seen in the face of a baby now, and he can never get it back. There is a gentle sadness and acceptance.
4. Which do you think are the most poetic lines? Why?
[Sample answer] The most poetic lines are: "Where did my childhood go? It went to some forgotten place, That's hidden in an infant's face, That's all I know."
These lines are beautiful because they use simple yet profound imagery. The idea that childhood is 'hidden in an infant's face' suggests that every new baby carries the potential for childhood, but once you grow up, you can never reclaim it. The vagueness of 'some forgotten place' adds mystery. The short, resigned phrase 'That's all I know' captures the poet's acceptance of this universal truth – we all lose childhood, and we can only see it now in the faces of babies.
