Understanding the Text

1. List the steps taken by the captain (i) to protect the ship when rough weather began. (ii) to check the flooding of the water in the ship.
(i) Steps to protect the ship when rough weather began:
• Dropped the storm jib to slow the boat down
• Lashed a heavy mooring rope in a loop across the stern
• Double-lashed everything on deck
• Went through life-raft drill
• Attached lifelines and donned oilskins and life jackets
• Slowed the boat to eight knots by using only a small storm jib

(ii) Steps to check flooding of water:
• Stretched canvas and secured waterproof hatch covers across the gaping holes
• Connected an electric pump from under the chartroom floor
• Used hand pumps continuously (though they got blocked)
• Made repairs with hammer, screws and canvas
2. Describe the mental condition of the voyagers on 4 and 5 January.
On 4 and 5 January, the voyagers experienced extreme stress and desperation:
• After 36 hours of continuous pumping, they had only a few centimetres of water left, but their respite was short-lived.
• By 4 p.m., strong winds returned (40 knots) and seas grew higher.
• The weather deteriorated throughout the night, and by dawn on January 5, their situation was again desperate.
• The captain felt hopeless and dozed off from exhaustion. When he woke at 6 p.m., he thought they had missed the island.
• Mary and the captain sat together holding hands, feeling the end was very near.
3. Describe the shifts in the narration of the events as indicated in the three sections of the text. Give a subtitle to each section.
The narration shifts from the initial smooth voyage to the disaster and then to the struggle for survival and rescue.

Section 1 (Paragraphs 1-6): "The Calm Before the Storm"
Describes the preparation for the voyage, the family's dream, and the building of Wavewalker. It sets the context.

Section 2 (Paragraphs 7-17): "The Giant Wave and Its Aftermath"
Narrates the terrifying encounter with the 15-metre wave, the damage to the ship, injuries, and immediate survival efforts.

Section 3 (Paragraphs 18-30): "Hope and Rescue"
Focuses on the continuous pumping, the children's courage, the captain's navigation calculations, and finally sighting Ile Amsterdam.

Talking about the Text

1. What difference did you notice between the reaction of the adults and the children when faced with danger?
The adults showed fear, exhaustion, and desperation, while the children displayed remarkable courage and maturity. The children (Jonathan and Suzanne) did not panic. Sue hid her injuries, saying "I didn't want to worry you when you were trying to save us all." Jonathan asked for a hug and said they weren't afraid of dying as long as they were all together. The adults, on the other hand, felt the end was near and sat holding hands in despair.
2. How does the story suggest that optimism helps to endure "the direst stress"?
Optimism kept the crew going despite hopeless situations. The captain never gave up – he kept pumping, repairing, and calculating. The children's positive attitude (Sue's handmade card, Jonathan's hug) boosted morale. Even when the captain thought they had missed the island, the children's cheerful announcement "You found the island" restored hope. Optimism helped them endure the direst stress by focusing on solutions rather than despair.
3. What lessons do we learn from such hazardous experiences when we are face-to-face with death?
Such experiences teach us:
• The importance of courage and presence of mind in crises
• That teamwork and mutual support are crucial for survival
• Optimism and a positive attitude can overcome even the darkest moments
• Love and family bonds give strength to face death without fear
• Proper preparation and training are essential before undertaking risky adventures
4. Why do you think people undertake such adventurous expeditions in spite of the risks involved?
People undertake adventurous expeditions because of:
• The human spirit of exploration and discovery
• The desire to challenge oneself and push personal limits
• The thrill of achieving something difficult and memorable
• To follow childhood dreams (like the captain wanted to duplicate Captain Cook's voyage)
• The sense of accomplishment and the joy of overcoming adversity
• To create lasting memories with family

Thinking about Language

1. We have come across words like 'gale' and 'storm' in the account. Here are two more words for 'storm': typhoon, cyclone. How many words does your language have for 'storm'?
[This is a personal/language-based question. Sample answer in Hindi context:] In Hindi, words for 'storm' include: आंधी (aandhi), तूफान (toofan), बवंडर (bawandar), चक्रवात (chakravat - cyclone), समुद्री तूफान (samudri toofan - storm at sea). There are several regional variations as well.
2. Here are the terms for different kinds of vessels: yacht, boat, canoe, ship, steamer, schooner. Think of similar terms in your language.
[Sample answer in Hindi:] In Hindi, similar terms include: नाव (naav - boat), जहाज (jahaj - ship), किश्ती (kishti - small boat), नौका (nauka - yacht), स्टीमर (steamer), डोंगी (dongi - canoe). There are also specific terms like बजरा (bajra - barge) and पाल-नौका (paal-nauka - sailboat).
3. 'Catamaran' is a kind of a boat. Do you know which Indian language this word is derived from? Check the dictionary.
The word 'catamaran' is derived from the Tamil language. In Tamil, 'kattumaram' (கட்டுமரம்) means 'tied wood' (kattu = to tie, maram = tree/wood). It refers to a traditional boat made by tying logs together.
4. Have you heard any boatmen's songs? What kind of emotions do these songs usually express?
[Sample answer:] Yes, boatmen's songs are common in many cultures, especially in riverine areas like Kerala, Bengal, and Assam. These songs usually express emotions of longing, hope, love for the river, fear of storms, and sometimes sadness of separation from loved ones. They often have a rhythmic beat that matches the rowing motion.

Working with Words

1. The following words used in the text as ship terminology are also commonly used in another sense. In what contexts would you use the other meaning?
Hull: Ship's body – also the outer covering of a fruit or seed (e.g., rice hull).
Mast: Ship's pole for sails – also a tall pole on land for flags or radio antennas.
Boom: Horizontal pole for sail – also a loud sound (boom of thunder) or a period of economic prosperity (boom in business).
Stern: Rear of a ship – also strict or serious in manner (stern look).
Jib: A triangular sail – also a type of crane in construction.
Bunk: Bed on a ship – also nonsense or false talk (that's a load of bunk).
2. The following three compound words end in -ship. What does each of them mean?
Friendship: The state of being friends; mutual affection and trust between people.
Partnership: The state of being a partner in business or association; shared responsibility and profit.
Relationship: The way two or more people or things are connected; a bond or connection.
3. The following are the meanings listed in the dictionary against the phrase 'take on'. In which meaning is it used in the third paragraph of the account?
In the third paragraph: "There, before heading east, we took on two crewmen – American Larry Vigil and Swiss Herb Seigler – to help us tackle one of the world's roughest seas..."
Here, 'take on' means "to employ sb; to engage sb" (meaning 2: take sb on: to employ sb; to engage sb).

Things to Do

1. Given below is a picture of a yacht. Label the parts of the yacht using the terms given in the box: bow, cabin, rudder, cockpit, stern, boom, mainsail, mast.
Parts of a Yacht (from front to back):
• Bow – The front part of the yacht.
• Cabin – The enclosed living/sleeping area below deck.
• Mast – The tall vertical pole that supports the sails.
• Mainsail – The largest sail attached to the mast and boom.
• Boom – The horizontal pole at the bottom of the mainsail.
• Cockpit – The open area where the helmsman steers.
• Rudder – The underwater blade at the stern used for steering.
• Stern – The back/rear part of the yacht.

Ile Amsterdam (Amsterdam Island) – Key Facts:
• Location: South Indian Ocean, between southernmost parts of Australia and South Africa
• Coordinates: 37° 92' S, 77° 67' E
• Sovereignty: France (part of French Southern and Antarctic Lands)

3. Locate Ile Amsterdam on the world map.
Ile Amsterdam is located in the southern Indian Ocean, roughly halfway between Australia, Africa, and Antarctica. Its coordinates are approximately 37°S latitude and 77°E longitude. It lies about 1,500 km north of the Antarctic Circle and is one of the most remote islands in the world. It is part of the French Southern and Antarctic Lands.
