1. List the different types of religious practice that you find in your neighbourhood. Does this indicate freedom of religious practice?

In my neighbourhood, I can see various religious practices like:

  • Hindus performing aarti in temples.
  • Muslims offering namaz in mosques.
  • Sikhs singing Gurbani in gurudwaras.
  • Christians attending Sunday Mass in churches.

Yes, the existence of these different practices clearly indicates the freedom of religious practice in India.

2. Will the government intervene if some religious group says that their religion allows them to practise infanticide? Give reasons for your answer.

Yes, the government will definitely intervene.

Reason: The practice of infanticide (killing an infant) violates the Fundamental Right to Life. The State has a duty to protect the life of every citizen. Therefore, the government will intervene to stop this criminal act, even if it is claimed to be a religious practice.

4. Look up the annual calendar of holidays of your school. How many of them pertain to different religions? What does this indicate?

Our school calendar has holidays for various religious festivals like Diwali, Eid, Christmas, and Guru Nanak Jayanti.

This indicates that the Indian State respects all major religions equally and does not give importance to any single religion. It is a key feature of Indian secularism.

5. Find out some examples of different views within the same religion.

Different views often exist within the same religion. For example:

  • In Hinduism, some people worship idols (Saguna), while others believe in a formless God (Nirguna).
  • In Islam, there are two main sects, Shia and Sunni, who have different interpretations and practices.
  • In Buddhism, there are the Mahayana and Hinayana schools of thought.

6. The Indian State both keeps away from religion as well as intervenes in religion. Explain this idea.

This idea means the Indian State maintains a "principled distance" from religion.

  • Keeps away (Non-interference): The State respects religious sentiments and does not interfere in most religious practices. For example, it allows Sikhs to wear a turban (pugri) instead of a helmet.
  • Intervenes (Intervention): The State intervenes in religion to end harmful social practices that violate Fundamental Rights. For example, it banned the practice of untouchability to uphold the ideal of equality.
