Exercise Questions

1. Suppose you are in a dark room. Can you see objects in the room? Can you see objects outside the room? Explain.
In a dark room, I cannot see objects inside the room because there is no light to reflect from these objects to my eyes. However, if there is light outside the room (like sunlight or artificial light), I might be able to see objects outside through a window or opening, as light from those objects can reach my eyes.
2. Differentiate between regular and diffused reflection. Does diffused reflection mean the failure of the laws of reflection?
Regular Reflection: Occurs when light reflects from a smooth, polished surface like a mirror. The reflected rays are parallel and form a clear image.

Diffused Reflection: Occurs when light reflects from a rough or irregular surface. The reflected rays scatter in different directions, and no clear image is formed.

Diffused reflection does not mean the failure of the laws of reflection. At each point on the rough surface, the laws of reflection are still obeyed (angle of incidence equals angle of reflection). However, because the surface is uneven, the normals at different points face different directions, causing the reflected rays to scatter.
3. Mention against each of the following whether regular or diffused reflection will take place when a beam of light strikes. Justify your answer in each case.
(a) Polished wooden table: Regular reflection (smooth, polished surface)
(b) Chalk powder: Diffused reflection (rough, irregular surface)
(c) Cardboard surface: Diffused reflection (rough surface)
(d) Marble floor with water spread over it: Regular reflection (water creates a smooth surface)
(e) Mirror: Regular reflection (smooth, polished surface)
(f) Piece of paper: Diffused reflection (slightly rough surface)
4. State the laws of reflection.
The two laws of reflection are:
  1. The angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r).
  2. The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
5. Describe an activity to show that the incident ray, the reflected ray and the normal at the point of incidence lie in the same plane.
Activity:
  1. Fix a white sheet of paper on a drawing board.
  2. Place a plane mirror vertically on the paper and draw its outline.
  3. Draw a normal line perpendicular to the mirror at the point of incidence.
  4. Direct an incident ray at an angle to the normal and mark its path.
  5. Observe and mark the reflected ray.
  6. All three (incident ray, normal, and reflected ray) will be on the paper, showing they lie in the same plane.
  7. If you bend the paper along the normal line, the reflected ray disappears from view, confirming they must be in the same plane.

(a) Always
(b) Sometimes

Explanation: According to the first law of reflection, the angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection, regardless of the surface or conditions.
8. Image formed by a plane mirror is
(a) virtual, behind the mirror and enlarged.
(b) virtual, behind the mirror and of the same size as the object.
(c) real at the surface of the mirror and enlarged.
(d) real, behind the mirror and of the same size as the object.
Explanation: A plane mirror always forms a virtual image that appears to be behind the mirror. The image is the same size as the object and is laterally inverted.
9. Describe the construction of a kaleidoscope.
A kaleidoscope is constructed as follows:
  1. Take three rectangular mirror strips (about 15 cm long and 4 cm wide).
  2. Join them together to form a triangular prism (mirrored surfaces facing inward).
  3. Fix this mirror arrangement inside a cardboard tube slightly longer than the mirrors.
  4. Close one end of the tube with a cardboard disc having a small hole in the center for viewing.
  5. At the other end, place a circular glass plate touching the mirrors.
  6. Put small pieces of colored glass or beads on this glass plate.
  7. Close this end with a ground glass plate, allowing space for the colored pieces to move.
  8. Decorate the outer tube with colored paper.
When you look through the hole, multiple reflections create beautiful, symmetrical patterns.

The human eye is a complex optical instrument that works similarly to a camera. Light enters through the cornea, passes through the pupil (whose size is controlled by the iris), and is focused by the lens onto the retina. The retina contains photoreceptor cells (rods and cones) that convert light into electrical signals, which are then transmitted to the brain via the optic nerve.

11. Gurmit wanted to perform Activity 13.8 using a laser torch. Her teacher advised her not to do so. Can you explain the basis of the teacher's advice?
The teacher advised against using a laser torch because lasers can be harmful to eyes. Laser light is highly concentrated and can cause permanent damage to the retina if shone directly into someone's eyes. Even brief exposure to laser light can potentially cause vision problems or blindness. Regular torch light is much safer for such activities.
12. Explain how you can take care of your eyes.
To take care of our eyes:
  1. Read in proper light - not too dim or too bright
  2. Maintain proper distance while reading (about 25 cm)
  3. Never look directly at the sun or bright lights
  4. Eat vitamin A rich foods (carrots, green vegetables, fruits)
  5. Wash eyes with clean water if dust particles enter
  6. Avoid rubbing eyes vigorously
  7. Get regular eye checkups
  8. Use protective eyewear when needed
  9. Take breaks during prolonged reading or screen time
13. What is the angle of incidence of a ray if the reflected ray is at an angle of 90° to the incident ray?
According to the law of reflection, angle of incidence = angle of reflection.

If the angle between the incident ray and reflected ray is 90°, then:
Angle of incidence + Angle of reflection = 90°
Since angle of incidence = angle of reflection,
2 × Angle of incidence = 90°
Therefore, Angle of incidence = 45°
14. How many images of a candle will be formed if it is placed between two parallel plane mirrors separated by 40 cm?
When an object is placed between two parallel mirrors, theoretically, an infinite number of images are formed. This happens because each image in one mirror acts as an object for the other mirror, creating a series of reflections that continue endlessly. In practice, the images become progressively dimmer and eventually become too faint to see.
15. Two mirrors meet at right angles. A ray of light is incident on one at an angle of 30° as shown in Fig. 13.19. Draw the reflected ray from the second mirror.
Solution:
  1. The first reflection: Angle of incidence = 30°, so angle of reflection = 30°
  2. This reflected ray will hit the second mirror at an angle of 60° (90° - 30° = 60°)
  3. For the second mirror: Angle of incidence = 60°, so angle of reflection = 60°
  4. The final reflected ray will make an angle of 60° with the second mirror
The reflected ray from the second mirror will be parallel to the original incident ray but in the opposite direction.
16. Boojho stands at A just on the side of a plane mirror as shown in Fig. 13.20. Can he see himself in the mirror? Also can he see the image of objects situated at P, Q and R?
Boojho at A: He cannot see himself in the mirror because he is positioned outside the field of view of the mirror. His position is such that light from himself cannot reach the mirror and reflect back to his eyes.

Objects at P, Q, and R:
  • He can see the image of object at P if it is within the mirror's field of view
  • He can see the image of object at Q if it is within the mirror's field of view
  • He might see the image of object at R depending on its position relative to the mirror
Whether Boojho can see these images depends on whether the light from these objects reflects off the mirror and reaches his eyes.
