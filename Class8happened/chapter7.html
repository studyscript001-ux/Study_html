Comprehension Check - Part I

1. Why didn't he pay the school fees on the day he brought money to school?
He didn't pay the school fees because the teacher who collected the fees, Master Ghulam Mohammed, was on leave that day, so the fees would be collected the next day.
2. (i) What were the coins 'saying' to him?
(ii) Do you think they were misguiding him?
(i) The coins were tempting him to buy jalebis, suggesting that jalebis are meant to be eaten, money is meant to be spent, and he could use the scholarship money to pay the fees the next day.
(ii) Yes, the coins were misguiding him because they were encouraging him to spend money that was meant for his school fees on temporary pleasure, which could lead to problems later.
3. Why didn't he take the coins' advice? Give two or three reasons.
He didn't take the coins' advice because:
  • He considered himself a good boy and thought looking at things in the bazaar was a sin
  • The money was meant for school fees and fund
  • He was afraid of facing Master Ghulam Mohammed and Allah miyan if he spent the money
  • He knew Master Ghulam Mohammed would punish him by making him stand on the bench
4. (i) What did the oldest coin tell him?
(ii) Did he follow his advice? If not, why not?
(i) The oldest coin told him that if he spent the fees money on jalebis that day, he could pay the fees with the scholarship money he would get the next day.
(ii) Initially, he didn't follow the advice. He clenched the rupees in his fist and went home, deciding it wasn't right for a child of his status to stand in the bazaar eating jalebis.
5. He reached home with the coins in his pocket. What happened then?
When he reached home, the coins continued their attempts at persuasion. They kept speaking until their voices began to choke. When he sat on the bed, they began to speak again. When he went inside to have lunch, they began to shriek. Finally, fed up, he rushed out barefoot and ran towards the bazaar to buy jalebis.

Comprehension Check - Part II

1. (i) Why didn't he eat all the jalebis he had bought?
(ii) What did he do with the remaining jalebis?
(i) He didn't eat all the jalebis himself because he bought a whole rupee's worth, which was a large quantity.
(ii) He distributed the remaining jalebis to children in the neighborhood, standing on a chabutara and liberally giving them away like the Governor distributed rice on Independence Day.
2. "The fear was killing me." What was the fear?
The fear was that with every burp, he might bring out a jalebi or two, revealing that he had eaten jalebis. He was also afraid that if he didn't eat dinner, he would have to explain why, and if he pretended illness, the doctor might discover he had eaten too many jalebis.
3. "Children's stomachs are like digestion machines." What do you understand by that? Do you agree?
This means that children can digest food quickly and efficiently, even large quantities. While children generally have faster metabolisms than adults, I don't completely agree with this statement as overeating can cause digestive problems for anyone, including children.
4. How did he plan to pay the fees the next day?
He planned to pay the fees with the scholarship money he expected to receive the next day.
5. When it is time to pay the fees, what does he do? How is he disobeying the elders by doing so?
When it's time to pay the fees, he leaves school and goes to the railway station instead. He is disobeying the elders by crossing the railway tracks, which they had warned him never to do.

Comprehension Check - Part III

1. What was the consequence of buying jalebis with the fees money?
The consequence was that he couldn't pay his school fees the next day because the scholarship money wasn't distributed as expected. He had to skip school, sit near the railway tracks, and pray to God for help. Eventually, his absence from school was discovered, and he faced punishment at home.
2. His prayer to God is like a lawyer's defence of a bad case. Does he argue his case well? What are the points he makes?
Yes, he argues his case well like a lawyer. His points include:
  • He is a good boy who has memorized the entire namaaz and last ten surats of the Quran
  • He can recite ayat-al-kursi
  • He admits his mistake but mentions he didn't eat all jalebis himself - he shared with other children
  • He promises not to repeat the mistake
  • He argues that Allah's treasury has no shortage
  • He mentions that even the chaprasi takes money home, and he is the nephew of a big officer
3. He offers to play a game with Allah Miyan. What is the game?
The game is that he would walk to the signal and back, and during that time, Allah Miyan should secretly place four rupees under a big rock. When he returns, he would lift the rock and find the money.
4. Did he get four rupees by playing the game? What did he get to see under the rock?
No, he didn't get four rupees. Under the rock, he found a big hairy worm that curled and twisted toward him, making him scream and run away.
5. If God had granted his wish that day, what harm would it have caused him in later life?
If God had granted his wish, he might have never learned the importance of responsibility and consequences. He might have continued to believe that he could solve problems by simply asking God for help rather than through his own efforts. The author later realizes that if Allah provided everything for the asking, humans wouldn't have learned to make jalebis or develop other skills and would still be living in nests like birds.

Exercise Questions

1. Select and read sentences that show:
- that the boy is tempted to eat jalebis.
- that he is feeling guilty.
- that he is justifying a wrong deed.
Temptation: "Those fresh, hot jalebis coming out of the kadhai in the shop over there, they're not coming out for nothing."

Guilt: "I am a good boy. Don't misguide me or it won't be good for you. I get so much at home that I consider even looking at something in the bazaar a sin."

Justification: "Eat jalebis with the fees money and then pay the fees with the scholarship money. I thought that two and two added up to four and could never be five."
2. Discuss the following points:
- Is the boy intelligent? If so, what is the evidence of it?
- Does his outlook on the jalebis episode change after Class VIII? Does he see that episode in a new light?
- Why are coins made to 'talk' in this story? What purpose does it serve?
Intelligence: Yes, the boy is intelligent. He was among the most promising students, won a scholarship, and could recite many verses from the Quran. He also tried to logically justify his actions.

Changed outlook: Yes, his outlook changes after Class VIII. He realizes that if God provided everything for the asking, humans wouldn't have developed skills and would still be living primitive lives. He understands the importance of human effort and learning from mistakes.

Coins talking: The coins are personified to represent the boy's inner temptation and internal conflict. This literary device makes the story more engaging and helps readers understand the struggle between responsibility and desire that the boy experiences.
